On Romance

What properly makes the beloved into the beloved, from a phenomenological standpoint? Consider the following hypothetical:

You have a dear platonic friend who you love so much and in such a way that after a long difficult day, the only thing you really wish for is to go to them and simply be around them. Now by an act of divine providence, some time later you also become sexually attracted to them, and they to you, and you start having sex with them. This ensemble of affects and acts sustain themselves for many years, if not blossom further.

Would you call this person your beloved? A romantic partner? If not, why not? Something does seem to be amiss. If the only complaints are of the social variety: are they committed to you and you alone? do you live together? etc., please set them aside. They are nearly arbitrary to the phenomenology of love. One must also set aside for the time-being all the behaviors that arise when one is caught up in limerence or the like: the lies, the hyperventilation and the idealization. I am interested in what comes after, if it does; in what constitutes romance in the settled-down parts of a long, stable romantic endeavor.

Looking inwards (and based on my other conversations on this hypothetical), the answer seems to be: what's missing is a lack of romantic orientation. A way of looking and transfiguring the person inside your own head in a singularly-privileged manner. "That is my partner". The merging of self-identities and planning your life around/building your life with the person, if they occur, are simply downstream of this orientation. So what is this orientation? What is the look of love? What is it made of? Is it made up?

Consider two situations: a just-stabilized fruitful relationship (~1 year) and a long fruitful marriage (~20 years).

In the former situation, there is a newfound comfort which characterizes the love. If a lie has not been told to oneself early on, the look and the orientation have nascently emerged, and are manifest in a certain recognition. A (sub)conscious recognition that Eros, Philia and the centering behavior ("going back at the end of the day") have settled into the slightest amount of habit through the knowledge that these things will not be taken away. If one watches their mind during the subtle sigh that one makes when noticing their beloved read or bathe in these days and later, in that moment of slowing down and looking, they will realize that it is fundamentally that same recognition, just brimming up to the conscious surface. The recognition that the pieces are there.

In the latter situation maybe, through sheer repetition, the sex has ebbed out and the friendship, while still sweet, has also lost intensity. Not all of these situations are tragic and listless. Some of these couples still speak of a great love. What remains then to enforce this feeling? The look and the orientation! Yet now, there is nothing to recognize...

One gathers then, that the look of love, when separated from Eros, Philia and centering, is a container affect: not a "thing of its own" (how would one define this?), but rather something that arises emergently, like an enveloping haze, once the more concrete affects are in place. This leads yet again to a rather strange conclusion-question: in the case of the hypothetical, i.e- when the pieces are in place, love then may be a choice. It is possible, that Love be intended or anti-intended (I do not mean unintended). Furthermore, as we have already noted, this container can actually function as a proxy for the containee in at least one situation. Can it proxy in another? Can Love be intended when the pieces aren't there and have never been there? Put simply, if you really tried, could you make yourself fall in love with any person of your romantic/sexual orientation? Arranged marriages...

Excerpt from an unfinished story: "When we love a person" he said, "we don't become blind to their flaws. I'm also against that very insipid belief of loving someone "for" their imperfections. It always sounded very patronizing to me. It's not really clear to me what makes me love someone in the first place. Many things can be guessed of course, but only after you already love someone. It isn't really the sum of its parts, even if all the parts could be enumerated. I know they can't. I can say with certainty however, that it isn't blindness. In fact, when I love someone I notice them much more. I notice all the things wrong with them. So what? I don't love them "inspite of their flaws" either. I just love them. It's a net I throw over them. It's all-encompassing. I think in their loving innocence, people think that they are loving the flaws too. But this is only true symbolically and after the fact.
